Beat Sheet V3

March 13, 2026
Six movements. Standalone scenes. A man, his armor, and the kid he used to be.

The Film in One Breath

A 37-year-old man lives his daily life accompanied by a version of himself who can only handle the good times. The armor is charming, funny, and terrified of silence. One night, a locked room and a kid in a chair crack something open. The man doesn't need to become anyone. He just needs to stop performing.

Runtime: ~90 minutes
Tone: The Nusrat Principle — happy melodies about sad things. FUN. Warm. Aching.
Structure: Six movements. Not chapters. Each movement is a collection of standalone scenes that share a mood. Scenes can be shot, rearranged, and rewritten independently.
Rule: The studio is a mirror. Only Su's own face appears in it.

Su

The Man

37. Content alone. Overwhelmed by oneness sometimes. Withdrew from people to protect himself. The bleeding stopped but now nothing flows. Every relationship is at arm's length. Loves his mother but can't give her what she wants. Sees his own ego, observes it, still lets it win sometimes.

The Comedian

The Scar Tissue — Constant Companion

Born the night Su needed armor. Walks with Su everywhere. Riffs in autos, at chai stalls, during boring meetings. BOLTS from anything real. Will not enter the studio. Performing aliveness 24/7. Can make forty strangers laugh. Can't sit across from one person and be seen. Terrified — and that's why he's funny.

The Kid

Before the Amputation — Studio Guest (First + Last)

~20 years old. From before everything got complicated. Can feel everything without it being destroyed. Runs towards people, not away. Doesn't know he's a ghost. Thinks he's just visiting. When he shows up in the studio, it's not nostalgia — it's a confrontation. Because he can do the one thing Su has forgotten: feel loved without flinching.

The Older Su

The Mirror That Finished — Studio Guest (Later)

The version who made the film. Festivals. Reviews. Everything Su dreams about. But tired. Not triumphant. Proof that the problem was never about the film. He doesn't roast Su. He reads him like test results. The only version who walks in through the door — because the future has somewhere real to go.

The Comedian's Rules

  • He is ALWAYS with Su — auto, office, walks, chai, dates. The audience doesn't know he's not real at first.
  • He NEVER enters the studio. The amber room demands honesty. He can't.
  • He BOLTS from heavy moments — mother's call, real conversations, silence that lasts too long.
  • He has ONE moment where he tries to stay. Fights it. Leaves anyway. That's the scene where the audience sees: he's not fun. He's terrified.
  • He is never in serious conversation with anyone other than Su. He lets real Su handle the world.
  • He disappears at the end — not dramatically. He's just... not there anymore. Su notices the silence. It doesn't hurt.

Su's Voice — How Philosophy Enters the Film

  • Studio Recordings: Su talks into the mic. Not for a show. Not for anyone. Processing. Fragments of these recordings bleed over walking scenes, auto rides, mornings. Diegetic inner voice.
  • The Comedian's Riffs: The same truths, but deflected through humor. He says profound things wrapped in jokes — and Su doesn't laugh because he hears what's underneath.
  • Silence + Image: The Malick approach. Show the ceiling. The sky. The walk. Let the audience FEEL the oneness. No words needed for the deepest moments.
  • The Kid Just Being: He doesn't philosophize. He embodies it. He IS the truth Su is trying to articulate. "Everything is a concept, like tomorrow" — the Kid would never say this. He'd just run outside.
  • Integrity test: If a line feels like it belongs in a motivational video, cut it. If it feels like something you'd say at 2 AM looking at the ceiling, keep it.
MOVEMENT 1

The Loop

Warm. Rhythmic. The audience falls in love with Su's world before anything cracks.
1.1

Black — Sound of Breathing

QUIET

Black screen. Someone breathing. Not distressed — deep, steady, like after a cold shower. The breathing becomes a hum. The Lyrical Loneliness theme. Then image: water hitting tile. Su's back. The cold shower ritual.

Camera: locked on the shower wall. Water runs. We see his silhouette. No face yet. The sound carries everything.
The first thing we hear is Su alone. The first thing we see is a ritual. The film opens on a man who has built a life of routines to keep himself steady.
1.2

The Morning

HOME SU SOLO SHOOT

Su's apartment. The lived-in chaos of a man who lives alone and has opinions about it. Kettle. Phone check. The notepad on the desk — ink on skin metaphor, words on paper, the cheat sheet of thoughts.

No dialogue. No voiceover. Just the sounds of a morning. The camera lingers on small things: the kettle's whistle, the phone face-down on the table, the un-made bed that's been un-made the same way for years.

Tsai Ming-liang energy. Static shots. The beauty of a mundane morning when no one is watching.
What does YOUR morning actually look like? The real version, not the cinematic one. The 10 AM coffee. The notepad. The phone you don't check and then check. What is the FIRST thing you see when you open your eyes?
1.3

The Auto — First Time We See the Comedian

AUTO / DELHI STREETS SU COMEDIAN FUN

Su in an auto. The driver's playing loud Punjabi music. Su looks out the window — Delhi scrolling past. Then a voice beside him. The Comedian. Already mid-sentence. Riffing about the driver's music taste, about the traffic, about the auto's decoration.

The audience doesn't know yet that the Comedian isn't real. They think it's a friend. Two guys in an auto, shooting the shit. The Comedian is charismatic, fast, sharp. Su smiles but doesn't fully engage — like a man who's heard these riffs a thousand times.

Camera: over-the-shoulder, then the Comedian in frame, then back to Su. The auto's rearview mirror catches both of them — or does it only catch Su? Don't answer this yet. Let the audience figure it out.
The Comedian is introduced as a friend. The reveal that he's Su's defense mechanism comes later. For now, he's just the funniest person in the auto.
The Comedian's riffs here should be YOUR actual auto observations. The stuff you think but don't say. The driver staring at girls. The signal-breaking. The bizarre dashboard decorations. What makes YOU smile in an auto?
1.4

The Studio — Killing Time

STUDIO SU QUIET SOLO SHOOT

Evening. Su in the studio. Not recording anything. Flicking the mic, listening to the thud through the monitor. Adjusting the chair position. Moving the water glass. The rituals of a man who doesn't know how to begin, so he arranges the furniture instead.

He talks into the mic. Not a show. Not an entry. Just... thinking out loud.

Studio Recording (fragment) Something about love. About breathing. About how he's good company for a few hours but the person who can't be alone can never be happy with him. Not polished. Rambling. Real.

He gets up to leave. Turns the door handle. It doesn't move.

The studio recording becomes the film's inner voice. We'll hear fragments of THIS recording over later scenes — the walking, the auto rides, the ceiling-staring. But right now, we just see a man talking to himself in a room. And then a door that won't open.
1.5

The Locked Room — The Kid Appears

STUDIO SU THE KID WONDER

The door won't open. Su pulls. Calls someone. No answer. He stops — because the monitor is picking up sound. A rhythmic tapping. The same pattern he was tapping on the mic thirty seconds ago. Someone is tapping the other mic.

He looks at the camera viewfinder. In the frame: someone in the guest chair. Small. Turned away. A kid. The kid waves — not at Su, at the camera. Like he knows exactly where to look.

The Kid turns around. Grins. Touches everything — the mic, the lights, the cables. He's amazed. He doesn't know he's a ghost. He thinks this is the coolest room in the world.

The conversation that follows is NOT deep. It's fun. The Kid asks about the equipment. About what Su does here. About why there are two chairs. Su doesn't know how to answer. The Kid doesn't need deep answers — he just wants to know things.

Then someone opens the door from outside. The Kid is gone. The chair is empty. The memory card recorded nothing.

Amber light throughout. The Kid lit the same way as Su — the light doesn't know who's real and who's imaginary. Camera intimate: close-ups, hands on mic, the water glass, the Kid's face full of wonder.
The first studio encounter is LIGHT. Warm. Fun. The Nusrat melody before the Nusrat lyrics. The audience learns the rules of the locked room without any heaviness. The heaviness comes later.
The Kid's dialogue should be things a genuinely curious 20-year-old would say in a studio for the first time. Not philosophical. Not wise. Just excited. "Ye camera mein red light kyun nahi aa rahi?"
1.6

After the Kid — The Walk

DELHI STREETS / NIGHT SU COMEDIAN

Su walks out of the studio. Shaken. The Comedian is waiting outside. "Kya hua? Bhoot dekh liya?" Su doesn't answer. Walks. The Comedian walks with him. Tries to riff. Su isn't biting.

For the first time, the Comedian can't make Su laugh. He tries harder. Gets louder. Su just walks. The Comedian eventually shuts up. They walk in silence. This is the first crack — something happened in that room that the Comedian can't reach.

Long take. Two figures walking a Delhi street at night. One talking, one silent. Then both silent. The city around them is alive but they're in their own tunnel.
MOVEMENT 2

The Comedian's World

Fast. Bright. The funniest part of the film. But underneath, the origin of armor.
2.1

Black — Sound of Laughter

ENERGY

Black screen. Laughter. Not one person — a room full. The laughter builds, peaks, crashes. Then silence. Then a single voice: a comedian mid-set. The laughter swells again. Image fades in: the comedy club. Spotlight. Black curtain behind. A figure in shades. Su.

This movement is the Comedian's backstory — told in fragments. Not chronological. Not linear. Pieces of standup nights that add up to a picture of someone who was most alive on stage and most lost everywhere else.
2.2

Fragment — The Set

COMEDY CLUB SU (PAST) FUN NEEDS AUDIENCE

Shades on. Spotlight. Su doing a set. The material is sharp — observational, self-aware, the kind that makes people laugh because they recognize themselves. No before. No after. Just the set. The energy of a room that's with you.

We see fragments: the opener, a callback that lands, the crowd leaning in. The joy of being funny. The pure adrenaline of a room laughing at something you made up in your head at 3 AM.

Shot differently from everything else in the film. Handheld. Close. The spotlight burns. The shades reflect it. The faces in the audience are blurred (blurred faces rule). Just Su and the sound of laughter.
These need to be YOUR jokes. Real material. The security/insecurity opener. The government bit. Whatever makes you feel most alive on stage. The film needs to SHOW why the Comedian exists — because performing was genuinely the best part of Su's life.
2.3

Fragment — The Phone Guy

COMEDY CLUB SU (PAST) SHIFT

Mid-set. Someone's phone goes off. Su handles it — starts roasting the guy. The audience loves it. Su keeps going. Gets personal. Gets mean. The room is laughing but the guy isn't. Su can feel the line being crossed but the laughter is a drug and he can't stop.

The bit ends. Applause. But something is different. Su's smile has changed. The shades hide the eyes but the jaw is tight.

The Comedian's reflex anger — the one that isn't calculated, just fires. This is what Su hates about the Comedian. This is why he stopped. Not because he wasn't good. Because he was good at being cruel.
2.4

Tapri — After the Show

TEA STALL / NIGHT SU (PAST) WARM NEEDS FRIENDS

The tapri. Comedian friends. Chai in glass cups. The sound of laughter continues — but softer now, private, between friends. Su is one of them. His hands around the cup. His face relaxed in a way we haven't seen.

Blurred faces. Blurred hands. The focus is on Su — his eyes, his laugh, the way he leans back against the wall. The voices around him are warm and real.

Focus rack: Su's face sharp, everything else soft. The only time in the film where Su looks genuinely relaxed around other people. The tapri is sanctuary.
Studio Recording (bleeding in, faint) I am good company for a few hours. Maybe a night. But the person who can't be alone... they can't be happy with me.
2.5

Present — The Comedian in Daily Life

VARIOUS SU COMEDIAN FUN

A montage of moments — not a traditional montage, more like postcards. The Comedian at Su's desk making fun of the emails. The Comedian in the kitchen critiquing how Su makes chai. The Comedian walking alongside him, narrating the city like a nature documentary.

These are the good times. This is why the Comedian exists — he makes the loneliness bearable. He turns every empty moment into material. The audience loves him. That's important. They need to love him before they understand what he costs.

Write 4-5 of these moments as one-page sketches. What does the Comedian say when you're making chai? When you're stuck in traffic? When you see a couple fighting? These are YOUR observations but given to the Comedian's voice — faster, sharper, funnier.
2.6

The Comedian Tries to Stay

HOME / EVENING SU COMEDIAN SHIFT

Su's phone rings. Maa. Su answers. The Comedian is there — sitting across the room, legs up on the coffee table, flipping through a notebook. Normal.

Maa talks. We don't hear her clearly — just the tone. The weight. Su's face changes. The conversation is about the thing it's always about. Marriage. Coming home. Being normal.

The Comedian shifts. Uncrosses his legs. The riffing energy drops. He looks at Su. He wants to say something funny — you can see it forming behind his eyes. But the room is too heavy. The words don't come.

He gets up. Walks to the door. Stops. His hand on the frame. He's fighting it — the urge to bolt. For one second, he's trying to be real. To stay in the room where something hurts.

He leaves. The door closes softly. Su is alone on the phone with his mother.

The almost-staying. The one time the audience sees that the Comedian isn't just fun. He's terrified. And that's why he's funny. Being funny is his only defense mechanism — and when it fails, he has nothing.
This scene is the emotional core of the Comedian's character. The phone call doesn't need dialogue from Maa — just her TONE and Su's face. What does your face do when your mother talks about the thing you can't give her?
2.7

Fragment — 1:30 AM in the Rain

DELHI STREET / RAIN / NIGHT SU (PAST) ACHE SOLO SHOOT

The fragment that explains everything. A different night. Younger Su waited outside a comedy club for five hours. 1:30 AM. Never got a spot. The rain started. He stood there. Everyone else had gone home. The autos had stopped running.

He walked home. In the rain. Alone. No Comedian beside him — because the Comedian doesn't exist yet. This is the BEFORE. The version of Su who could feel the rain and just feel the rain, without turning it into a bit.

This should be the most beautiful shot in the film so far. Rain on empty Delhi streets. One figure walking. No umbrella. No rushing. The Lyrical Loneliness hum under the rain sound. This is NOT sadness. This is the lyrical loneliness itself — the thing Su is named after in this film.
The Comedian was born sometime between this rain walk and the phone guy set. Somewhere in between, Su decided feeling things without armor was too dangerous.
2.8

The Studio Door — The Comedian Won't Enter

STUDIO CORRIDOR + STUDIO SU COMEDIAN QUIET

Present. Su walks toward the studio. The Comedian is with him, riffing about something. They reach the door. Su goes to open it. The Comedian slows down.

"Chal na, kya karega andar baith ke. Chai peete hain."

Su opens the door. Steps inside. Looks back. The Comedian is standing in the corridor. He won't cross the threshold. He shrugs — casual, like it doesn't matter. But his feet don't move.

Su sits. Looks at the empty chair. The amber light. The two mics. From outside, the Comedian's voice: "Tera problem pata hai kya hai? Tu har jagah meaning dhundhta hai."

Su doesn't respond. He sits in the silence. After a while, he gets up and leaves. The Comedian is waiting outside: "Chal, chai peete hain." Like nothing happened.

The studio demands honesty. The Comedian cannot be honest. This scene tells the audience everything about who the Comedian is in one image: a man standing outside a door he can't walk through.
MOVEMENT 3

The Loneliness of Being Loved

Lyrical. Soft. The film slows down. Su navigates the world of love, dates, family — the places where armor doesn't work.
3.1

The Date — Modern Love

RESTAURANT / EVENING SU COMEDIAN BITTERSWEET

Su on a date. A modern one — app-matched, both pretending to be more interesting than they are. The Comedian sits at the next table, watching. He's quiet — not riffing, just observing. Giving Su space.

The date is fine. Pleasant. Forgettable. Su is present but not there. He's doing all the right things — asking questions, smiling, nodding. But his eyes are watching the candle. The girl notices. She says something. We don't hear it. We see Su's face trying to recalibrate.

After, walking to the auto stand. The Comedian finally speaks: "She was nice." Su: "Haan." The Comedian: "Tu bhi nice tha." The way he says "nice" — it's not a compliment. It's a diagnosis.

The date shot through glass — reflections, the restaurant's warm light making it look like a still life. The Comedian at the other table is in the background, out of focus. We can see him. She can't.
3.2

The Arranged Meeting

CAFÉ OR FAMILY HOME SU COMEDIAN UNCOMFORTABLE

A girl his family set him up with. Different energy entirely. Both know why they're here. She's nice. Educated. The kind of person his mother describes on the phone. Su is so far away from this kind of life that the distance is almost physical.

The Comedian, for once, is respectful. He's not roasting the situation. He's sitting back, giving space, like even he knows there are lines. This shows a different shade — the Comedian isn't always a clown. He knows when to be quiet. He just chooses not to, most of the time.

Su isn't against love or marriage. He's against performing it. The difference between "I don't want to love" and "I can't love the way you need me to" — that's the gap the film lives in.
Have you actually been on an arranged meeting? What was the most uncomfortable moment? The most human moment? The one that made you think "if I were a different person, this would work"?
3.3

Walking Alone — Love as Breathing

DELHI STREETS / LATE NIGHT SU LYRICAL SOLO SHOOT

Su walks. No Comedian tonight — he bolted hours ago. The city is half-asleep. Street dogs. Shuttered shops. The distant hum of a generator. Su's footsteps on asphalt.

Studio Recording (from an earlier night, bleeding in) Love is like breathing. In the moment of true devotion there is no separation between the devoted and the devotee. They are one. That is love for me. The spontaneous feeling not polluted by any kind of thought...

We hear this over the walk. Not as narration — as something Su said once in an empty room, now floating over an empty street. The words are real. The setting makes them feel lived, not preached.

Long, slow tracking shot from behind. Su getting smaller as the street stretches. Or: static shot, Su walks through frame and out. The camera stays on the empty street. The audio continues. The absence IS the image.
In the moment of true devotion there is no separation. Su knows this. He FEELS this sometimes — the oneness with the universe, the overwhelming beauty of just existing. But he can't feel it with a person. He can feel it with a ceiling. With a street. With rain. Not with a person. That's what was amputated.
3.4

The Phone Call — Mother

HOME / EVENING SU ACHE SOLO SHOOT

A proper phone call this time. Not long — mothers and sons who've stopped knowing how to talk to each other don't talk long. We hear her voice but not the words. We see Su's face. The effort of being a son who can't give his mother what she wants.

He says "haan" a lot. He says "theek hai." He says "baad mein baat karte hain." He hangs up. Sits. Stares at the phone. The silence in the apartment is the loudest thing in the film.

The mother doesn't need to be in the studio. This phone call IS the mother scene. A man who loves his mother but their love has become a negotiation neither of them wanted.
3.5

Studio Recording — The Ego

STUDIO SU HONEST SOLO SHOOT

Su at the mic. Late. Alone. Not expecting anyone tonight — the room isn't locked, the door is open. He's just talking.

Studio Recording (live, we see him say it) I see my ego. I observe it. I see its ebbs and flow. I understand it. And I still let it win. I'm getting better. But there's no point I'm trying to reach. There is no point to reach, actually. Everything is a concept. Like tomorrow.

He laughs at his own words. Not a performance laugh. The laugh of a man who caught himself being profound and found it funny. He drinks water. Gets up. Leaves the mic on. The red light stays.

The deepest lines in the film should make Su LAUGH, not cry. Because he's not sad. He's a man who sees clearly and finds the clarity funny. The Nusrat Principle at work — the truth is heavy, the delivery is light.
MOVEMENT 4

The Mirror That Finished

Colder. Quieter. The film's center of gravity. Su meets the version who did everything he dreams about — and it didn't help.
4.1

The Comedian Weakens

VARIOUS SU COMEDIAN SHIFT

The Comedian's energy is dropping. His jokes land slower. His presence is less constant — he's not in every auto ride anymore. Sometimes Su looks to his right and the seat is empty. The Comedian shows up later, like he overslept. His riffs are recycled.

Something is changing. The studio visits — the Kid, the nights of talking into the mic — are doing something to Su that the Comedian can't counter. Su is becoming more honest. And honesty is the Comedian's poison.

The Comedian doesn't die in a dramatic scene. He fades. Like a radio station slowly going out of range. The signal was strong once. Now it crackles.
4.2

The Older Su Appears

STUDIO SU OLDER SU WEIGHT

Studio. Door locks. Su isn't scared anymore — he knows how this works now. He sits. Looks at the empty chair. Looks at the glass of water. Then he sees it — a face in the water's reflection. He looks up. The Older Su is there.

The Older Su is different from every other guest. He came through the door — the only one who uses the door, because the future has somewhere real to go. He looks around the studio like he recognizes everything. Touches the mic the way you touch a scar.

The conversation is not a roast. Not a therapy session. It's two men who are the same person separated by time. The Older Su is tired. Not broken — just done. He made the film. Festivals. Reviews. Everything. And it didn't fix the thing Su thinks it'll fix.

Dialogue seeds (not final) "Tu film bana liya?" / "Ban gayi. Sab ban gayi." / "Toh?" / "Toh kya? ... Ek aur cheez finish ho gayi. Bas."

The Older Su's "bhaagna" version: "Tu time se bhag raha hai. ... Mujhse bhag ke kahan jayega?"

Finishing the film won't save Su. Not-finishing the film won't save Su either. The pattern is the problem, not the project. The Older Su knows this because he lived it.
This is the hardest conversation to write because it's the one you're most afraid of. What does the successful version of you feel like? Is he happier? Or is he just... done? What would you ask him if he showed up tonight?
4.3

After the Older Su — The World Feels Different

VARIOUS / DAY SU SHIFT

The next day. Something is different. Su moves slower. Not sadder — more present. He notices things: the light on the building across the street. The sound of the pressure cooker from a neighbor's kitchen. The way the auto driver's hands rest on the steering wheel at a red light.

The Comedian is barely there. A shadow in the corner. A voice that starts a sentence and doesn't finish it. Su doesn't miss him yet. But the silence is new.

The camera breathes for the first time. Longer holds. More air between shots. The film itself is slowing down because Su is slowing down.
MOVEMENT 5

The Unraveling

Raw. The armor comes off. Not violently — like a man undressing at the end of a long day.
5.1

Su Goes to the Studio Voluntarily

STUDIO SU QUIET SOLO SHOOT

For the first time, Su goes to the studio not because the door locked, not because he's killing time. He goes because he wants to sit in that chair. He turns on the mic. Talks. Really talks — not rambling, not performing. Just a man being honest with the only room that ever demanded it from him.

Studio Recording (the real one) What he says here should be from the entries. The real words. The ones that made him cry. Not cleaned up for cinema. Raw.
5.2

The Comedian's Last Try

HOME / EVENING SU COMEDIAN BITTERSWEET

The Comedian shows up. Full energy. Like the old days. He's got material — new stuff, good stuff. He's trying. He riffs about the arranged marriage girl. About the date. About how Su sat in the studio talking to himself like a "pagal aadmi."

Su watches him. There's no anger. No confrontation. Just clarity. Su sees through the performance now. He sees the terror underneath. The Comedian was never protecting him from the world — he was protecting him from feeling.

Su doesn't say any of this. He just looks at the Comedian the way you look at someone you've outgrown. With love. With sadness. With the knowledge that they can't come where you're going.

The Comedian feels it. The riff dies. He sits down. For a moment — just a moment — he's not performing. He's just a man sitting in a room with himself. Then he gets up and walks out. This time, he doesn't say "chal, chai peete hain." He just leaves.

The Comedian doesn't die. He isn't killed. He isn't confronted or defeated. He's loved. And the love is what makes him leave. Because love is the one thing the scar tissue can't survive.
5.3

Real Moments Break Through

VARIOUS SU WARM

A sequence of small real moments. Su calls his mother. Brief but warmer. He doesn't say anything different — but the tone has shifted. She notices. Doesn't say anything. Mothers notice.

Su watches a film alone. Something hits him. He cries. Not dramatically — his eyes just fill. He doesn't wipe them. He lets it happen. (First time in the film Su lets himself feel without armor or analysis.)

Su at his desk. Writing. Actually writing — not planning, not noting, not infrastructure. Words on paper. The pen moves.

These scenes should be BRIEF. Ten seconds each. No dialogue. Just images of a man who's starting to let things through.
MOVEMENT 6

The Return

Still. Clear. The Nusrat melody and the Nusrat lyrics finally become one thing.
6.1

The Kid Comes Back

STUDIO SU THE KID STILL

Studio. The door locks. Su sits. He doesn't look at the empty chair with dread or excitement. He just waits. He knows.

The Kid is there. Same kid. Same grin. But something is different — not in the Kid. In Su. Su can see him now. Really see him. Not as a ghost or a mechanism or a metaphor. As the version of himself who never needed armor because he didn't know the world would hurt him.

The conversation is short. The Kid says something devastatingly simple. Not philosophical. Not wise. Something a child would say that makes 37 years of living feel like a long detour.

You said it yourself: "so childish and so elementary that all the learned experience of his life becomes irrelevant." You'll know the line when you hear it. It'll come from one of your 40-minute sessions. It might come from a place that has nothing to do with film or art — it might be something your mother said, or something you said to a stranger in an auto, or something the kid inside you says when you're staring at the ceiling at 3 AM. The line is already in you. You just haven't recorded the right entry yet.
6.2

Su Walks Out

STUDIO → DELHI STREETS → DAWN SU BREATHING

Su walks out of the studio. The Comedian is not waiting outside. The corridor is empty. Not ominously — just empty. The absence is quiet. Like a room after music stops. You don't miss the music. You just notice the silence is okay.

He walks. Doesn't take a cab. Doesn't take an auto. Walks. Delhi in the hours between night and morning. Street dogs. A chai stall opening. A sweeper. The city breathing.

The Lyrical Loneliness hum. But it doesn't sound lonely anymore. It sounds like breathing. Like the devoted and the devotee being one. Like a man and his city and the 3 AM air between them.

Long. Patient. No rush to end. The camera walks with him. Not behind him. Beside him. Like a companion that doesn't need to talk.
6.3

The Last Image

END

This is yours to decide. Some seeds:

Su walks until he reaches his building. Goes up the stairs. Opens the door to his studio. Sits. Adjusts the mic. Looks at the empty chair. Hits record. The red light comes on. He looks at the camera. Not a comedy look. Not a scared look. Just: seen. Black.

OR: Su walks. Dawn breaking. A real kid — a stranger — runs past him on the empty road. Running for the joy of it. Su watches. The kid disappears around a corner. Su starts walking again. Not running. But not standing still. Black.

OR: Su reaches home. Opens the door. The apartment is the same. But he sees the ceiling the way you see it when the oneness hits — not a ceiling, just sky painted over. He lies down. Stares up. The hum continues. Black.

The last image of the film is a promise. What do you want the audience to carry out of the theatre? Is Su alone? Is he moving? Is he finally still? Record an entry about this. The answer is in your body, not your head.

How to Use This Beat Sheet

  • These are scenes, not a shooting script. Each scene is a standalone short film. Write whichever one you can SEE right now. Not the one the structure demands.
  • The movements are moods, not chapters. You might shoot scenes from Movement 3 and Movement 1 on the same day. The order is emotional, not chronological.
  • Every scene marked SOLO SHOOT can be shot with just you and a camera. That's more than half the film. Start there.
  • The "YOUR TURN" boxes are your homework. Not writing assignments — speaking assignments. Take each one into a 40-minute session. Record it. The script comes from those recordings, not from this document.
  • The Comedian needs a performer. Unless you play him too (different energy, same face — GenAI). Figure this out early.
  • The Kid needs a performer. Same question. A real 20-year-old who looks like you? Or GenAI younger Su? Both have trade-offs.
