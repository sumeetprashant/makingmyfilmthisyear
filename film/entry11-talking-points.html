The Locked Room — The First Night, Beat by Beat

Here's how it might feel. Continue from these lines:

SU (calling out) Bhai? Koi hai? ... Hello? Silence. He pulls out his phone. Calls someone. It rings. No answer. He calls again. Same.

POINT 09 OPEN

The Shades Problem — How He Looks at the Camera Through Shades

You caught the loophole: shades in all standup scenes is locked, but the 4th wall look needs eyes. If he's wearing shades on stage, the audience can't see the break.

Three paths:

1. The shades come down. One specific moment on stage where something happens (the phone guy, a heckler, a line that doesn't land) and he pushes the shades up to his forehead. His eyes are naked for the first time. He looks at the camera. The shift is physical and visible — the persona comes off.

2. The reflection. We never see his eyes directly. But we see them reflected — in a glass, in a phone screen, in someone's glasses in the front row. The eyes are always there, just mediated. Like Su himself — present but never fully exposed.

3. Light through the shades. The spotlight is strong enough that we CAN see his eyes through the lenses — barely, like shadows. It's not a clear look. It's a suggestion of a look. The audience feels it more than sees it.

Tonight: which one? The shades coming down is the most dramatic. The reflection is the most clever. The light-through-lenses is the most Su — visible but hidden. Your call.
