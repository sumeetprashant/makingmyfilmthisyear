Quick Feedback on the Second LLM

What It Got Right

  • The Act 1 spine is clean: morning → auto → work excellence → night ritual → indulgence → price → warm moment → fight → no Comedian → Kid sighting. Ten beats. That's your Act 1.
  • "Do not write the philosophy conversation now" — correct. Act 2 or 3.
  • "One specific failure is better than general fog" — correct. The indulgence price needs to be a concrete fuckup, not a vibe.

What It Got Wrong

  • It pushed back on Himachal in Act 1. You've decided. The warm moment IS Himachal. The missed call from Maa, the Comedian pushing Su to ignore, nature as the space where they're in sync — that's not "protecting a mood." That's a scene you can see. Trust it.
  • It said "Himachal in every act is a dangerous pattern." No. Himachal in every act with a different companion (Comedian, alone, both) is one of your strongest structural instincts. It gives the film a geographic heartbeat. Act 1 Himachal = peace. Act 2 Himachal = loneliness. Act 3 Himachal = integration. That's not a system — that's a triptych.
  • Ten talking points is too many. You said you're tired. You need five max.

What It Missed Entirely

  • The date WITH the Comedian in Act 1 — you just said this and it matters. If the audience sees the Comedian helping Su navigate a girl (probably badly, probably hilariously), then the arranged marriage WITHOUT the Comedian becomes structurally devastating. The Comedian's absence is felt because we saw what his presence looked like.
  • The festival ending is NOT Su watching a screen. You corrected this: lanyard in focus, Comedian and Kid out of focus, film ends. That's the real ending. The three of them exist in the same frame for the first and only time. Su has arrived somewhere. His inner selves are at peace. No dialogue. No screen light on face. Just focus pulling from a lanyard to two figures playing. Black.

ONE — Su at Work. The Specific Moment.

This is the hole in the film right now. You've said Su is indispensable but never shown it.

Here's what to talk through: a podcast shoot is about to go sideways. The host is stiff. The guest is bored. The energy in the room is dying. Su steps in — not as director, not as boss, but as the guy who makes the question list. He whispers something to the host. Or he rewrites a question on the spot. Or he makes a joke to the guest that loosens them up. Whatever it is — it saves the shoot.

Now: where did that come from? The humor? The timing? The confidence to be absurd in a professional setting? That's the Comedian. Su doesn't acknowledge it. But the audience should feel it. Maybe Su cracks a line and the Comedian, sitting in the corner, grins — like a coach watching his player use the move he taught him.

You said it yourself: "Without the comedian Su will be a loner sitting and jerking off in his room. He gave him voice and then confidence in his voice." Show that. Not by explaining it. By one moment where Su is brilliant and the Comedian is invisibly the reason.

TWO — The Date WITH the Comedian.

This is new and important. Act 1, Su meets a girl — not arranged, just something organic. Maybe through friends, maybe a random encounter. The Comedian is THERE. He's not being subtle.

Talk through: what does the Comedian do while Su is trying to talk to this girl? Does he whisper roasts in Su's ear? Does he rate the conversation? Does he mock Su's nervousness? Does he actually help — toss Su a line that makes the girl laugh?

The key: the Comedian makes Su more interesting than Su actually is. Su's natural state is withdrawn, polite, watching the candle. The Comedian's interference — even though Su finds it annoying — is what makes him funny, unpredictable, alive. The girl isn't falling for Su. She's falling for the version of Su that the Comedian co-pilots.

This matters because when the arranged marriage meeting happens WITHOUT the Comedian in Act 2, Su is just... Su. Polite. Present. Empty. And the girl across the table can feel it. And so can the audience.

THREE — Himachal, Act 1.

This is the warm scene. No tussle. Su and the Comedian in nature.

Talk through these specific things:

  • How do they get there? Train? Bus? A spontaneous decision?
  • What's the Comedian like in the mountains? Is he quieter? Does nature do the entertaining so he doesn't have to? Or does he point out things — a bird, a cloud, a weird tree — with the same energy he points out things in Delhi but gentler?
  • The missed call from Maa. The Comedian pushes Su to ignore it. What does he say? Not the exact words — the gesture. A head shake? A hand wave? A "let it go"?
  • On the way back, another missed call. Does Su look at the phone differently this time? Is the warmth from the trip still insulating him?
  • What's the ONE image? Two figures on a trail? At a dhaba? Looking at a valley from a bus window? Pick the frame you see.

FOUR — The Mother Call and the Fight.

This is your Act 1 ending. The chain:

The earlier Maa call was chill. Just checking in. But at the end she drops it — "ek ladki se mil lo" or whatever she says. Su deflects: "Maa, kaam hai." Hangs up. Fine. Normal. The Comedian doesn't bolt — it wasn't that heavy.

But then: the arranged marriage becomes concrete. Maa calls again. This time it's not a suggestion — it's a plan. A date, a girl, a family. Su's politeness cracks. He says something to his mother that he'll regret. The specific thing — what is it? What does a son say to a loving mother when he can't be what she needs? Not cruel — just the truth delivered without padding. That's worse than cruelty.

Then the Comedian. He thinks it's no big deal. "Bhai, jaa ke mil le, kya hoga." Maybe teases. Marriage jokes. The ones Su hates. And Su turns on him. Not the playful "what the fuck" from the office scene. Something real. Something that hits the space between the Comedian's ribs where the kid used to be.

What's that line? You don't need to write it now. But describe the FEELING of it. What part of the Comedian does Su attack? His never being serious? His running from everything? The fact that he thinks love is a gamble? What's the wound?

The Comedian doesn't show up the next morning. Empty auto seat. The film changes.

FIVE — The Comedy Club and the First Kid Sighting.

Su without the Comedian. He can't function the way he used to. He's not funny with clients. He's not quick. The voice is gone and he didn't even know it was borrowed.

He calls his old comedian friends. Goes to the club. Not to perform — to sit. In the green room, or at a table in the back. He sees the comedians. Maybe sees a fragment of the Comedian performing — a memory, an imagining, a wish.

He's reaching for his soul.

And his soul responds.

The next morning. Auto ride. Looking outside. A kid on the street. Waving.

Describe: what does Su's face do? Not recognition — not yet. Just that flicker. "I've seen this face." The auto doesn't stop. The kid is gone. But the feeling stays.

Day 2: earlier in the ride. The kid is there. Closer. Su notices.

Day 3: the kid is in front of the auto. Gets in. Sits beside Su. Looks at him with the pure version of Su's own face. And Su looks at the camera — fucked up.

That's five. That's your Entry 12. Pick three and go.

Lanyard in focus. Comedian and Kid out of focus behind him. Film ends.
