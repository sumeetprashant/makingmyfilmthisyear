# Topic: AI Usage in the Film

> How AI is used. What's allowed. What's not. Why it matters.

---

## The Del Toro Principle (CORE)

Guillermo del Toro (video shared by Su, stored at `film/del-toro.mp4`):

> The process of making art is what gives the result its dignity. AI can generate outputs but it can't struggle. Without struggle, there's no meaning.

Su shared this because it articulates his deepest fear about using AI in his own film: that the result won't carry the weight of human effort. **This is not a passing concern — it's a foundational anxiety.**

## What AI IS Used For

| Use | Purpose | Example |
|-----|---------|---------|
| VFX | Cost-cutting. Can't afford traditional VFX | Environmental elements, dream sequences |
| Costumes/Hair/Makeup | Creating different versions of Su for studio guests | Dupatta for Maa, younger look for Young Su |
| Visual enhancements | Cinematic quality on zero budget | Color, atmosphere, subtle digital set extensions |
| Film-within-the-film | The surreal/fantasy segments where AI can "go wild" | Where Su imagines the film he'd make with money |
| Face/character transformation | Studio guests are Su's face with different characteristics | GenAI face modification for different characters |

## What AI is NOT Used For

- **Replacing creative decisions.** AI doesn't decide what the film is about or how it should feel
- **Replacing the human process.** The writing, the performing, the editing — that's Su's work
- **Generating the "real life" footage.** The daily routine, auto rides, walks, conversations — these are shot for real

## Sequencing Rule

1. **The film opens with REAL footage.** This establishes authenticity and earns the audience's trust
2. **AI-generated elements come LATER** — after the audience is invested and trusts the visual language
3. **When AI elements appear, they should feel INTENTIONAL** — a deliberate artistic choice, not a budget limitation

## The "Slop" Problem

- Su is worried (rightly) that AI-generated content might look like "AI slop" — the recognizable artificiality of generated imagery
- Solution: **restraint and purpose.** Use AI where it serves the story (dream sequences, character transformations), not as a shortcut
- The film-within-the-film segments are WHERE AI goes wild — the audience knows these are fantasy/dreams, so the artificiality becomes part of the visual language
- The opening should NOT rely heavily on AI if it risks looking unconvincing
